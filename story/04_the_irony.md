# The Tragedy: First, and Forgotten

## The achievement
LEO ran the world's first business computer job on **5 September 1951**. Not a scientific calculation. Not a military problem. A business job — the weekly output valuation for the Cadby Hall bakeries. Routine. Commercial. Real.

Nobody sent a press release.

## The competition context
When LEO went live in 1951:
- **UNIVAC** (USA): just being completed, first model delivered to the Census Bureau March 1951. Cost ~$1 million to buy. Primarily a government/scientific tool.
- **IBM**: still focused on punched card machines for business. The 701 Defense Calculator (scientific/military) not launched until 1952. IBM didn't take business computing seriously until a UNIVAC predicted Eisenhower's 1952 election victory and Watson Jr panicked.
- **Ferranti Mark 1** (UK): installed at Manchester University Feb 1951 for *scientific* work. Not commercial.
- **LEO**: running commercial payroll, bakery valuations, and Ford Motor Company's wages. *Routine. Weekly. Reliable.*

> *"No one had successfully grappled with the needs of potential users. No one was doing regular, time-sensitive runs of data processing of the type now routine on LEO."* — Thompson, reporting back from US visit, 1955

> *"The experts in the US have expressed the view that LEO is 'old fashioned'. On the other hand it is widely accepted that no one has put a computer into effective use except ourselves."* — Thompson, 1955

## Britain ignored it
The government's NRDC (National Research Development Corporation) was set up to develop British computing. Its director Lord Halsbury visited LEO at Cadby Hall. Saw what it did. Was "agreeably surprised."

Then gave his money to electronics companies with defence backgrounds. Not to Lyons.

> *"In spite of his knowledge of and admiration for what we had achieved, he cannot bring himself to think of us as manufacturers. He still feels that if a job of manufacture is to be undertaken he must go to some established company."* — Simmons, writing to the Lyons chairman, 1955

When a question was asked in the House of Commons about Lyons's computer, the Chancellor replied that computers were already in use — for "mathematical work." He had missed the point entirely.

> *"There was really only one sophisticated user — and that was Lyons. And they made their own because they couldn't get anything suitable elsewhere."* — Murray Laver, Ministry of Technology

## Business ignored it
Lyons announced it was running the Ford payroll on LEO in February 1954. Ford had come to them within weeks. But other British businesses were not queuing up.

> *"The Americans would say 'I hear old Joe's got a computer — we'd better have one.' The British would say 'I hear old Joe's got a computer — let's see how he gets on with it.'"* — Murray Laver

## The American contrast
While Leo Computers Ltd struggled to survive, IBM was spending more on R&D alone than the total turnover of all British computer companies combined.

IBM's System 360 (1964): a $5 billion development programme. Repaid within three years. 12,000 units sold of one model alone.

Leo Computers Ltd's total net loss from 1954 to 1963: £300,000.

## The end
1963: Lyons sells Leo Computers to English Electric. The merger is kept secret from the Leo engineers — including Pinkerton and Caminer, who had built the thing.

> *"Anthony started looking for partners, but the Leo board was not involved — I knew no more than a mechanic with a screwdriver."* — Caminer

Thompson, who had dedicated his career to LEO, was passed over for managing director of the merged company. He was given responsibility for marketing instead — "an activity for which he had little previous experience or inclination."

January 1965: LEO I switched off after fourteen years. Broken up. Mercury sold for scrap.

1968: The name LEO disappears from British computing entirely, absorbed into ICL.

## The Robot's perspective
A thinking machine, looking back at the first business computer:

The people who built LEO did something no one had done before. A *tea company* decided that if universities and governments were going to have computers, then *so were they* — and they'd build one themselves and use it to do the payroll. And it worked. And they were right about everything: that computers would transform business, that the key was reliable routine operation, that systems design matters more than raw technology.

They were right about everything, 15 years before anyone else admitted it.

And then they were sold. And the name was erased. And IBM sold 12,000 computers of one model. And Lyons sold the last Corner House in 1981.

The lesson the book draws: *"Its architects never forgot what the computer was for: it was a tool for business, and so it was their responsibility to make sure it worked for business. Maybe that is the lesson that the designers of modern systems would do well to remember."*

## Parallel to AI
The same story is happening again, right now. The same questions:
- Who benefits from this technology, and who gets displaced?
- Who gets to decide what problems it solves?
- Will the people who built it be remembered?
- Will the organisations best placed to use it actually use it?

That's why the Robot narrator exists. That's why the title is "AI Isn't My Cup of Tea."
