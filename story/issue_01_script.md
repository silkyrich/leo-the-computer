# Issue 1 Script: "The Weight of Numbers"
**Setting:** Cadby Hall, Hammersmith. 1923–1947.
**Theme:** The problem. Human beings doing machine work. One man's twenty-year dream.

---

## Story So Far box (bottom of page 1, left):
Robot Narrator: "1935. London. Britain's largest catering company is drowning in arithmetic. This is how the computer was born — in a room full of people doing machine work."

## This Issue box (bottom of page 1, right):
"A room of clerks. A farthing's profit. And one man who sees what nobody else can see."

---

## PAGE 1 — Layout H (cover, lion column left)

**Panel 1** (top-right under masthead, ~30% height)
EXTERIOR WIDE SHOT. Cadby Hall, Hammersmith, 1935. Grand red-brick Edwardian factory-fortress. Workers arriving in overcoats, morning fog, a Lyons delivery lorry. Sign above the gates: J. LYONS & CO.
Caption: *"Cadby Hall, Hammersmith. 1935."*
Caption: *"Britain's largest catering empire. Two hundred teashops. Thirty thousand employees. And a problem nobody could solve."*

**Panel 2** (middle-right, ~25% height)
INTERIOR. The Checking Department. Wide shot receding to a vanishing point. Rows of young women at Burroughs adding machines, separated by wooden partitions. Green eyeshades, stacks of bills. The mechanical rhythm of 300 machines.
Caption: *"Three hundred clerks. One job: check the waitresses' bills. All day. Every day."*

**Panel 3** (lower-right, ~20% height)
CLOSE-UP. A hand cranking a Burroughs calculator, paper roll printing. The other hand holds a Lyons teashop bill. On the desk beside the machine: a single small coin — a farthing.
Caption: *"The profit on one cup of tea. One farthing."*

---

## PAGE 2 — Layout K (Issue 1 · Page 2)

**Panel 1** (top-left, half width)
A young woman at her Burroughs calculator. Her expression blank, automatic. Fingers moving without thought. She has been doing this for four hours. She does not look up.
Caption: *"Total attention required. Zero intellectual reward."*

**Panel 2** (top-right, half width)
A male supervisor in a dark suit walks the aisle between desks. The women do not look up. He checks a paper, moves on. The wooden partitions between the women are deliberately placed to prevent conversation.
Caption: *"Partitions had been erected between the desks to reduce distraction."*

**Panel 3** (middle, full width)
The Checking Department from above. Full scale. Through a doorway at the back: a second room, just as large, just as full of clerks.
Caption: *"The Checking Department was one of three central clerical offices."*

**Panel 4** (bottom-left, half width)
An office boy carrying a stack of locked leather bags — teashop bills delivered that morning. He is sorting them by number on a long table.
Caption: *"Two hundred and fifty shops. Every morning. Locked leather bags."*

**Panel 5** (bottom-right, half width)
JOHN SIMMONS stands at the end of an aisle, watching the room. Early 30s, slim, round wire-rimmed spectacles, neat dark hair, jacket and tie. His expression: not contempt, not pity — something colder and more precise. A man looking at a problem and already solving it.

---

## PAGE 3 — Layout K (Issue 1 · Page 3)

**Panel 1** (top-left, half width)
THE BOARDROOM. Simmons stands before the board. A chart shows teashop numbers growing year-on-year.
Speech bubble (board member, off-panel): *"Can we open more shops?"*
Speech bubble (Simmons): *"Only if we hire more clerks."*
Speech bubble (board member): *"And the cost?"*
Caption: *"More shops. More clerks. More cost. Smaller margins."*

**Panel 2** (top-right, half width)
CLOSE ON Simmons's notebook. His careful handwriting: "The day will come when machines will do this work." Underlined. The date beside it: 1923.
Caption: *"He had written those words twelve years ago."*

**Panel 3** (middle, full width)
A LYONS CORNER HOUSE dining room. Busy, grand, chandeliers. A NIPPY in her black-and-white uniform moves between tables, tray held high, perfectly composed. This is Lyons at its best — warm, human, elegant.
Caption: *"150 million meals a year."*
Caption: *"The Nippy who served them had no idea what it cost to count them all."*

**Panel 4** (bottom-left, half width)
SIMMONS'S OFFICE, late evening. Single desk lamp. Everyone else has gone. He's reading a thick report. Through the window: London at night.
Caption: *"He had rationalised everything he could. It was never enough."*

**Panel 5** (bottom-right, half width)
CLOSE ON his hand, making a note at the foot of the page. He writes one word and underlines it: "Automatic."

---

## PAGE 4 — Layout K-final (Issue 1 · Page 4)

**Panel 1** (top-left, half width)
FLASHBACK: 1923. Simmons's first week. An older clerk shows him to a desk. Beside him: a man standing at a Dickensian high desk, entering figures by hand into a leather ledger. Simmons stares. This is where it begins.
Caption: *"1923. His first week."*

**Panel 2** (top-right, half width)
1935. The SYSTEMS RESEARCH team — six people around a table, flow charts on a board. More efficient, better organised. But still people. Still human arithmetic at the core.
Caption: *"Twelve years of rationalisation. The most efficient clerical operation in the world. Still not enough."*

**Panel 3** (middle, full width)
1945. A LYONS TEASHOP without Nippies. Self-service now. A woman serves herself from a counter. The room is the same. The uniform is gone. The Checking Department is gone. The job Simmons spent twenty years dreaming of automating no longer exists.
Caption: *"The war took the Nippies. The Checking Department closed."*
Caption: *"The very job he'd dreamed of automating had been abolished."*

**Panel 4** (bottom, full width)
1947. SIMMONS'S DESK. A memo arrives — he picks it up. Fragments visible: "...advanced office machinery... United States... Thompson and Standingford..." His expression shifts: not excitement, not yet — but something long-patient that is beginning to stir.
Caption: *"1947. A memo. Two men. A ship."*
Caption: *"And somewhere in America — the answer."*
