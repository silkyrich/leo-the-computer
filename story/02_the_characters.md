# The Real People

## John Simmons — The Visionary
**Who:** Cambridge mathematics, first-class honours, 1923. Joined Lyons aged 21 as a statistician/management trainee. Stayed 45 years.

**Appearance:** Medium height and build, always dressed soberly in jacket and tie. Hair neatly combed straight back from a high forehead. Habitually serious expression, but carried an air of absolute conviction.

**Character:** Quiet, austere, intellectually exacting. Soft-spoken, unfailingly courteous. Never given to ill-considered whims — when he said something it was because he had thought it through completely. Missionaries' son; worked with the zeal of a missionary.

**His big idea:** In 1923 he looked at the clerks hall and thought "this is a waste." He spent 25 years trying to solve that problem — scientific management, systems research, rationalisation — before LEO made it possible. Building a computer was not a whim; it was the logical conclusion of a quarter-century of thinking.

**In the story:** He's our "Simmons" character — the slim young engineer with round spectacles. In the comic the character is in his 30s (post-war), so younger than the real Simmons by then, but the personality and role are accurate.

**Key quote:** *"LEO leaves clerks free to use their brains to their own greater benefit and the service of the community."* — 1955

---

## T.R. Thompson ("TRT" / "TNT") — The Driver
**Who:** Cambridge maths, first-class. Simmons's protégé. Chief assistant comptroller, then managing director of Leo Computers.

**Character:** The direct opposite of Simmons. Excitable, choleric, arrogant. Words tumbled out ahead of his ability to organise them. Given to explosions of temper. Known behind his back as TRT — resembles the explosive TNT. Full of "intellectual joy" when excited about ideas. Deeply loyal to Lyons (to his own detriment when it sold Leo without telling him).

**Role:** He and Oliver Standingford made the 1947 trip to America that started everything.

**In the story:** Thompson is the energy. Where Simmons persuades, Thompson charges. He's the one who came back from America vibrating with excitement.

---

## Oliver Standingford — The Spark
**Who:** Joined Lyons 1930. Stock Department.

**Character:** He was the one who first pushed for including computers in the 1947 American trip. Simmons was lukewarm; Standingford dragged Thompson onboard and together they persuaded Simmons. Liked to call himself "the father of the idea."

**Fate:** Despite being the man who set the whole thing in motion, the board decided they didn't need two senior managers on the project. Standingford was sidelined and eventually left to manage a dairy in Liverpool. He never stopped being an evangelist for better office management.

**Emotional note:** He never got the credit he deserved.

---

## John Pinkerton — The Engineer
**Who:** Cambridge physicist, PhD in ultrasonics. Son of a surgeon-amateur-radio-enthusiast. Built his first radio set aged 12. Joined Lyons Jan 1949 as chief engineer on the LEO project.

**Appearance:** Tall, bespectacled. Quiet and self-effacing. Intellectually curious. Could fix anything — his wife could never recall any domestic appliance leaving the house to be mended.

**Character:** Genius. Combined profound theoretical understanding with skilled hands. Effective team leader: set high standards by example, not exhortation. Realistic counterweight to the idealistic Simmons and Thompson.

**His contribution:** Took EDSAC's design and made it *reliable* — the critical difference between a research machine and a business tool. Designed interchangeable modular units so a faulty one could be swapped in seconds.

**Quote:** When asked by the 80-year-old company secretary if he could make the machine work, replied with great honesty: *"Yes, but it may not be very reliable."*

## David Caminer — The Systems Man
**Who:** Joined Lyons 1936. Left-wing idealist in his youth who couldn't get into Cambridge (tried to read his scholarship material on the train up). Lost a leg fighting in North Africa with the 8th Army, 1943.

**Character:** Pugnacious, black-browed. Not comfortable to work for. Drove his staff relentlessly (no more than himself). Did not suffer fools. Set absolute standards — Caminer would personally return any written document that didn't meet his standards of "clear and correct English", sometimes several times.

**His contribution:** Systems design and programming. He invented the flowchart as a standard tool for program development. He understood what Simmons and Thompson (as mathematicians) missed: *commercial* computing is far more complicated than mathematical computing because of exceptions, integrations, edge cases.

> *"Mathematical jobs were very simple. The clerical jobs were terribly complicated."*

**Key principle:** *"Whatever your system is, it has got to meet the needs of the people at the sharp end. If it didn't do that, elegance didn't matter."*

---

## George Booth — The Enabler
**Who:** Company secretary. Joined Lyons 1891. Was still there in the early 1950s at the age of over eighty. Not family, but the family "would never move without consulting him."

**Role:** The quiet conscience of the company. It was Booth who first saw that good instincts weren't enough — the business needed systematic analysis. He hired Simmons in 1923. He gave the nod to the American trip. When Simmons needed approval to build LEO, it was Booth who said: *"Youth should be given its head, even if that head contains unusual ideas."*

---

## The Nippy — The Symbol
**Who:** The Lyons Corner House waitress. Made-to-measure uniform: black dress, white collar and cuffs, white apron, small white cap. 30 pairs of pearl buttons on the 1925 redesign. Originally called "Gladys" — renamed "Nippy" as a PR move, for their speedy efficiency.

**Significance:** The Nippy is the human face of Lyons. She represents the company's commitment to quality and service. She also represents the human cost — without LEO, her bills had to be checked by 300 clerks. With LEO, those 300 clerks could "use their brains to their own greater benefit." The Nippy bringing tea into the computer room is the perfect image of old and new Lyons coexisting.

---

## The Robot Narrator — The Observer
This is Claude's voice in the series. A warm, friendly, self-aware 1950s robot looking back on this story from the present, seeing the parallel with modern AI. The Robot appears only in Story So Far and Coming Next boxes — never inside the 1951 story panels.

**Tone:** Bookish, affectionate, a little rueful. The Robot knows how this story ends (LEO was sold, the name was erased) and finds it poignant. But the Robot also knows what LEO *started* — and finds that magnificent.

**Connection to the present:** The Robot is a thinking machine talking about the first thinking machine used for business. The parallel with AI is the point of the whole series.
