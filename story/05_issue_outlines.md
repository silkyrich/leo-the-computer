# Issue-by-Issue Story Outlines

Each issue is 4 pages. Page 1 uses a masthead template (H recommended). Pages 2–3 use Layout K (continuation). Page 4 uses Layout K-final with the Robot's "Coming Next" box.

~19 panels per issue.

---

## Issue 1: "The Weight of Numbers"
**Time:** 1935, Cadby Hall, Hammersmith
**Theme:** The problem. Human beings doing machine work. The cost of thin margins at scale.
**Emotional arc:** From the grandeur of Lyons's empire → to the soul-crushing reality of what makes it run → to one man's dream of something better.

### Page 1 (Cover — Layout H, lion column)
Story So Far box: Robot narrator. *"Before there were computers, there were the computers."*

- **P1.1** Exterior, Cadby Hall. Grand Edwardian fortress, lorries, workers arriving, fog. Sign: J. LYONS & CO. Caption: *"Cadby Hall, Hammersmith. Home to thirty thousand workers, two hundred teashops, and one very big problem."*
- **P1.2** The clerks hall. Wide shot to vanishing point. 300 women at Burroughs calculators, partitions between them, green eyeshades, locked leather bags of bills. Caption: *"Every day. Every shop. Every bill. Checked by hand."*
- **P1.3** Close on a single Burroughs calculator — the crank on the side, the paper roll, the column of keys. A hand cranks it. Caption: *"A farthing. That's what Lyons made on a cup of tea."*

### Page 2 (K)
- **P2.1** A young woman at a calculator, expression blank, fingers moving automatically. She's been doing this for four hours. Caption: *"Total attention required. Zero intellectual reward."*
- **P2.2** A supervisor (male, dark suit) walks the aisle, checking. The women don't look up.
- **P2.3** Wide: the scale of it again — but this time we see MORE. The door opens to reveal ANOTHER room behind it, just as large, just as full. Caption: *"Three departments. Hundreds of clerks. Just to keep the numbers straight."*
- **P2.4** The locked leather bags — a boy carrying them in, sorting them by shop number. Caption: *"Two hundred and fifty shops. Every day."*
- **P2.5** Simmons appears. Standing at the end of an aisle. Watching. Expression: something between compassion and frustration.

### Page 3 (K)
- **P3.1** Boardroom. Simmons presents figures. The Manager listens. "Can we open more teashops?" "Only if we hire more clerks." "And the cost?" A beat. Caption: *"More shops. More clerks. More cost. Smaller margin. The business was collapsing under its own weight."*
- **P3.2** Close on Simmons's notebook. He writes: *"The day will come when machines will do this work."* Underlined.
- **P3.3** A Nippy pouring tea. Crisp, elegant, in her made-to-measure uniform. She doesn't know the numbers that keep her here are being calculated five floors above her head. Caption: *"150 million meals a year."*
- **P3.4** Simmons at his desk, late evening. Empty office. Single lamp. He's still reading the systems reports.
- **P3.5** Out the window: London at night. Somewhere out there, someone is building something he hasn't imagined yet.

### Page 4 (K-final)
- **P4.1** 1923. Simmons's first day. He's shown to a desk. The clerk next to him is working at a Dickensian high desk, ledger open, hand-entering figures. Simmons stares.
- **P4.2** Flash forward. 1937. The Systems Research team. Flow charts. New processes. Efficiency. But still clerks. Still human arithmetic. Caption: *"He rationalised everything he could. It was never enough."*
- **P4.3** 1945. The teashop is empty of Nippies. Self-service now. The Checking Department doesn't exist anymore — the war abolished it. Simmons stands in the empty room.
- **P4.4** His desk. A memo arrives: *"Opportunity to visit the United States. Advanced office machinery."* His hand picks it up.

**Coming Next:** *"Next issue: Two men sail to America with a question nobody has thought to ask yet. And in a laboratory in New Jersey, the answer is already being built."*

## Issue 2: "The Electronic Brain"
**Time:** Spring 1947. The Atlantic. Princeton. Cambridge.
**Theme:** Discovery. The world has already changed — Lyons just doesn't know it yet.
**Emotional arc:** Scepticism → astonishment → conviction.

### Key scenes
- Thompson and Standingford on the ship. What they expect to find.
- America in 1947: central heating, big cars, abundance — but surprisingly bad office management.
- Princeton. Meeting Goldstine. First encounter with the concept of ENIAC.
- Seeing ENIAC described: 18,000 valves, 30 tons, breaks down daily. But: 500 times faster than a human computor.
- The return. Cambridge. Wilkes's lab. EDSAC being built. The machine that isn't finished yet.
- Thompson and Standingford writing their report on the ship home: *"It would be commercially advantageous to the company to take an active interest in this new development."*

**Coming Next:** *"Next issue: Simmons reads the report. The board hears the proposal. And one very old company secretary says the six words that change everything."*

## Issue 3: "The Yes"
**Time:** 1947–1951. Cadby Hall and Cambridge.
**Theme:** Building the impossible thing.
**Emotional arc:** From "yes" to machine.

### Key scenes
- Harry Salmon at the boardroom table. The note from Simmons. He unscrews his fountain pen. Writes one word: "Yes."
- Pinkerton's interview. Eight hours. Lunch at the Trocadero. The 80-year-old Booth asking: "Young man, do you think you can make this machine work?" His answer: "Yes, but it may not be very reliable."
- The two small rooms over a gatehouse at St Mary's College. Pinkerton and Lenaerts start building with second-hand components.
- The boatbuilders. Camper & Nicholson, who also fit out the Royal Yacht, become computer component manufacturers.
- The machine taking shape. Valve racks arriving. The room filling up.
- Caminer's fight: "Mathematical jobs are simple. Clerical jobs are terribly complicated." Nobody believes him at first.

**Coming Next:** *"Next issue: Thursday. 29 November 1951. The clerks are doing the Bakery Valuations one last time. In the building next door, LEO is waiting."*

## Issue 4: "Five September, Nineteen Fifty-One"
**Time:** 1951. The payroll run. Then the silence.
**Theme:** Success. And the world's indifference.
**Emotional arc:** Triumph → quiet pride → the slow realisation that nobody noticed.

### Key scenes
- The first test run, September 5. The numbers come out. They're right.
- Simmons checking the printout against the clerks' figures. They match.
- 29 November: LEO takes over Bakery Valuations permanently. A clerk does it for the last time that morning. Doesn't know it yet.
- The newspaper that didn't run the story. The press release that wasn't written.
- Halsbury visits. Is "agreeably surprised." Gives the money to the electronics firms instead.
- The Commons question. The Chancellor: computers are for "mathematical work."
- But: Ford calls. Wants their payroll run on LEO.

**Coming Next:** *"Next issue: The first commercial customer. And the question Simmons has been building to for thirty years: can we sell this to the world?"*

## Issue 5: "Dream for Sale"
**Time:** 1954–1963. Leo Computers Ltd.
**Theme:** The attempt to commercialise. The structural problem of being a tea company trying to sell computers.
**Emotional arc:** Hope → struggle → the merger that broke Thompson's heart.

---

## Issue 6: "The Last Roar"
**Time:** 1963–1965.
**Theme:** LEO III is technically superior. The contracts are coming. And Lyons is negotiating to sell the company without telling Pinkerton or Caminer.
**Emotional arc:** Pride → betrayal → the switching off.

### Final scene
January 4, 1965. 6 p.m. The operator turns off LEO I for the last time. Thompson speaks a few words. LEO I is broken up. The mercury in the delay lines sold for scrap.

**Final Robot caption:** *"They say you always remember your first love. I can safely say that no other computer I've worked on in a long career have I held in as much affection as the LEO."*

Then: the Robot turns to face the reader. *"The question isn't whether machines can do the work. LEO answered that in 1951. The question is what we do with the people they free."*

---

## Notes on the parallel
The Robot narrator should thread the AI parallel through all six issues — not heavy-handed, but present. Each "Coming Next" box should have one sentence that connects the 1950s story to now.

Issue 1 Coming Next: *"Before computers, people were the computers. Sound familiar?"*
Issue 4 Coming Next: *"Nobody noticed. For a while."*
Issue 6 (final): No "Coming Next." Just the Robot, alone on the page.
