# Key Dates and Events

## 1894
J. Lyons & Co. opens its first teashop at 213 Piccadilly. Queues form immediately. Within a year it has to double capacity.

## 1909
First Lyons Corner House opens in Coventry Street. Seats 5,000 people. Four restaurants on upper floors, each with a live band.

## 1923
John Simmons (Cambridge maths, first class) joins Lyons as statistician/management trainee. Sees the clerks hall for the first time and is appalled.

## 1925
Simmons visits IBM in the United States. Decides punched card technology isn't suitable for Lyons's needs. Returns unconvinced.

## 1930s
Simmons builds the Systems Research department. Begins systematic analysis of every clerical job. The operation becomes "the showcase of the world." But they've hit a wall — the clerical operation is as efficient as it can be with human beings. Something revolutionary is needed.

## 1935
Systems Research introduces microfilm (Recordak) for the Wholesale Bakery Sales Department. First commercial use of microfilm in the world. Clerks drowning in paper; microfilm eliminates the filing problem.

## 1939–1945
World War Two. Lyons teashops serve tea through the Blitz (70 destroyed by bombs). Cadby Hall packs ration boxes. Lyons managers run a munitions factory at Elstree. Nippies disappear from teashops — wartime labour shortages force conversion to self-service. The Checking Department — the specific job Simmons dreamed of automating — ceases to exist.

## 1945–1946
Staff return from the war. David Caminer returns minus a leg. Jack Edwards (Lyons chief electrical engineer) tells Oliver Standingford: "electronics will be the technology of the future for office machines."

## Spring 1947
Simmons sends Thompson and Standingford to America to study office machines. Standingford insists they also visit computing research. They sail across the Atlantic.

They visit Princeton and meet Herman Goldstine — godfather of ENIAC. They see what electronic computing can do.
They visit Cambridge on their return and see Maurice Wilkes beginning to build EDSAC.

## October 1947
Thompson and Standingford submit their report. Key finding: *"It would be commercially advantageous to the company to take an active interest in the new development."* Translation: build a computer.

## 1947–1948
Lyons enters into agreement with Cambridge. They will help fund EDSAC; in return Wilkes will help them build a business version. Lyons secondes Lenaerts to Cambridge.

## 8 May 1949
EDSAC runs its first successful programs at Cambridge.
Chairman Harry Salmon receives a note from Simmons. Passes it round the table. Unscrews his fountain pen. Writes one word on a piece of paper and sends it back: **"Yes."**

Work begins on LEO I at Cadby Hall.

## September 1948
Simmons places a cryptic advertisement in the journal *Nature* for "a graduate electronics engineer aged 25–35." John Pinkerton applies. Pinkerton joins Lyons January 1949.

## 1950
LEO development team formally assembles in WX Block, Cadby Hall. Pinkerton leads engineering. Caminer leads systems and programming. Thompson leads overall.

Boatbuilder Camper & Nicholson (who also fit out the Royal Yacht) become part-time computer component manufacturers when they turn out to be cheaper than the original subcontractor.

## 5 September 1951
**LEO I runs the world's first business computer job** — Bakery Valuations, calculating the value of one week's output from Cadby Hall bakeries. Runs successfully as an experiment.

## 29 November 1951
**LEO takes over Bakery Valuations from the clerks permanently.** The first time a computer runs a routine office job as an integrated part of business operations. Nobody sends a press release.

## 1954
Ford Motor Company approaches Lyons. Wants their Dagenham plant payroll run on LEO. The first commercial customer.

LEO also runs the national tax tables for the Inland Revenue overnight — a job that previously took weeks by hand.

## 4 November 1954
Leo Computers Ltd incorporated as a separate subsidiary. Simmons wants to spread the LEO philosophy to British business. The board is cautious but agrees.

## 1957
LEO II/1 delivered. Four times faster than LEO I.

## 1958
LEO II/3 installed at Stewarts & Lloyds steelworks. It will run for thirteen years continuously.

## 1963
English Electric merges with Leo Computers to form English Electric Leo (EEL). Thompson is devastated to be passed over for MD. The Leo people feel sold out. The name begins to dissolve.

## January 1965
**LEO I switched off for the last time.** It had worked faithfully for Lyons for fourteen years. Raymond Thompson pays tribute to a machine that "seeded a great industry." Then LEO I is broken up. The mercury in the delay lines sold for scrap.

## 1968
English Electric Leo Marconi is absorbed into ICL. The name LEO disappears entirely from the British computing industry.

## 1998
ICL — the company that eventually swallowed LEO — is acquired by Fujitsu. Now operates as Fujitsu Services.

---

## The bitter irony of the timeline
- LEO ran the world's first business computer job in **1951**
- IBM launched its first serious business computer range in **1954–55**
- By **1964** IBM had sold 12,000 of its 1401 model alone
- The entire British computer industry was merged into ICL in **1968** and sold to a Japanese company in **1998**
- LEO's creators were never properly celebrated. The LEO people hold reunions. They have a website. They remember.
