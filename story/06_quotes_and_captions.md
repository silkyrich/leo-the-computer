# Quotes, Captions, and Ready-to-Use Lines

## Direct quotes from the source (use verbatim or near-verbatim)

*"A waste of human intelligence."* — Simmons, seeing the clerks hall for the first time

*"Machines would be invented which would be capable of doing all this work automatically."* — Simmons, 1930s

*"LEO leaves clerks free to use their brains to their own greater benefit and the service of the community."* — Simmons, 1955

*"The electronic office, as we conceive it, is essentially a users' machine."* — Simmons

*"Youth should be given its head, even if that head contains unusual ideas."* — George Booth (aged 78), on approving the American trip

*"Yes, but it may not be very reliable."* — Pinkerton, asked if he could make LEO work (to the 80-year-old company secretary)

*"Mathematical jobs were very simple. The clerical jobs were terribly complicated."* — Caminer

*"Whatever your system is, it has got to meet the needs of the people at the sharp end. If it didn't do that, elegance didn't matter."* — Caminer

*"Nobody has put a computer into effective use except ourselves."* — Thompson, 1955

*"The Americans would say 'I hear old Joe's got a computer — we'd better have one.' The British would say 'I hear old Joe's got a computer — let's see how he gets on with it.'"* — Murray Laver, Ministry of Technology

*"There was really only one sophisticated user — and that was Lyons. And they made their own because they couldn't get anything suitable elsewhere."* — Murray Laver

*"I don't suppose we shall ever again keep a computer in service as long as this one."* — On the decommissioning of LEO II/3 after 13 years, 1971

*"They say you always remember your first love. In terms of things mechanical — and the LEO was certainly that — I can safely say that no other computer I've worked on in a long career have I held in as much affection as the LEO."* — Former LEO operator, from the LEO Computers Society website

*"Its architects never forgot what the computer was for: it was a tool for business, and so it was their responsibility to make sure it worked for business."* — Epilogue

## Caption lines (written in the comic's voice)

**On scale:**
- *"Cadby Hall, Hammersmith. Home to thirty thousand workers, two hundred teashops, and one very big problem."*
- *"150 million meals a year. A farthing profit each."*
- *"Two hundred and fifty shops. Every day. Every bill. Checked by hand."*
- *"Three departments. Hundreds of clerks. Just to keep the numbers straight."*

**On the drudgery:**
- *"Total attention required. Zero intellectual reward."*
- *"At least on an assembly line, you could let your mind wander."*
- *"Every week. Two thousand wages. Calculated by hand. By people."*
- *"Before there were computers, there were the computers."*

**On the decision:**
- *"The heads around the table nodded. Harry Salmon unscrewed the cap of his fountain pen."*
- *"He wrote one word on a piece of paper and sent it back."*
- *"Yes."*

**On the build:**
- *"Two small rooms over a gatehouse. Second-hand components. One genius engineer."*
- *"The boatbuilders who also fitted out the Royal Yacht became, briefly, computer manufacturers."*
- *"6,000 valves. 2,000 feet of wire. Three years."*

**On the payroll run:**
- *"5 September 1951. The figures came out. They were right."*
- *"Nobody sent a press release."*
- *"On 29 November 1951, LEO took over Bakery Valuations from the clerks. It was the first time a computer ran a routine office job as part of a working business."*
- *"No one in Whitehall noticed. IBM hadn't heard of it. The world carried on."*

**On the ending:**
- *"4 January 1965. 6 p.m. The operator turned off LEO I for the last time."*
- *"The mercury in the delay lines was sold for scrap."*
- *"The name LEO disappeared from British computing in 1968."*
- *"The Lyons Corner Houses closed. The last teashop shut its doors in 1981."*
- *"Cadby Hall was demolished and sold to developers in 1984."*

## Robot narrator voice — Coming Next boxes

The Robot speaks directly, warmly, with self-awareness. He knows he's a machine talking about machines.

**Issue 1 (end of problem-setting issue):**
*"Next issue: Two men sail to America with a question nobody has thought to ask yet. And in a New Jersey laboratory, the answer is already being built."*

**Issue 2 (end of discovery issue):**
*"Next issue: Simmons reads the report. The board convenes. And one very old company secretary says the six words that change everything."*

**Issue 3 (end of build issue):**
*"Next issue: Thursday. 29 November 1951. The clerks are doing the Bakery Valuations for the last time. In the building next door, LEO is waiting."*

**Issue 4 (end of triumph-and-silence issue):**
*"Next issue: Ford calls. The Inland Revenue calls. The world is beginning to notice — just not the world that was supposed to."*

**Issue 5 (end of commercialisation issue):**
*"Next issue: LEO III is the best computer in Europe. And Lyons is about to sell it without telling the people who built it."*

**Issue 6 (final issue — no Coming Next, just the Robot):**
*"The question was never whether machines could do the work. LEO answered that in 1951. The question is what we do with the people they free. We're still working on that one."*

## Historical facts that work as captions
- The Checking Department had 300 clerks doing one job: checking waitresses' bills
- The 1925 Nippy uniform had 30 pairs of pearl buttons
- Lyons served 150 million meals per year
- The company supplied 30,000–40,000 small orders to grocers every week
- The payroll program had to calculate the exact coins needed to fill each pay packet
- Caminer invented the programming flowchart as a standard tool
- The Ford payroll program at Dagenham was written by four people in weeks
- LEO I worked for 14 years continuously
- LEO II/3 at Stewarts & Lloyds ran for 13 years without replacement
- John Pinkerton was described by Maurice Wilkes as "the most able of all the industrial computing engineers at that time"
- The boatbuilding company that made LEO components also fitted out the Royal Yacht
- A telephone call to a C&N director at Cadby Hall turned out to be from Buckingham Palace
