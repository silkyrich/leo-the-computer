# The Problem: Why LEO Had to Exist

## The core paradox
Lyons sold tea and buns for a few pence each. The profit on a single transaction could be as little as **one farthing** — barely 4p in today's money. Yet the company served **150 million meals a year** across 250+ teashops, supplied **30,000–40,000 small orders per week** to grocers, ran Corner Houses seating 5,000 people at a time, and employed over 30,000 people.

At that scale, even tiny inefficiencies in recording and analysing transactions could wipe out the profit entirely. The margin was so thin it could be destroyed by bad paperwork.

> *"The modest profit margin on each purchase could easily be wiped out if the clerical work involved in recording and analysing all those transactions was inefficient."* — Ch.1

## The clerical machine
To manage this volume, Lyons built a vast clerical infrastructure:
- **300 clerks in the Checking Department alone** — mostly young women straight from school
- Their single job: add up waitresses' bills from 250+ teashops, check them against cash takings
- Tools: Burroughs mechanical calculators with a crank on the side, adding machines, paper rolls
- Office boys kept them supplied with bills delivered in locked leather bags from the teashops each morning
- Senior male clerks and managers stalked the aisles ensuring every head stayed bent to its task
- Partitions erected between desks to reduce distraction

## The drudgery — Simmons sees it
John Simmons arrived at Lyons in 1923. He walked the checking department and saw this:

> *"All he saw was a waste of human intelligence. Punching a Burroughs calculator could be worse drudgery even than unskilled factory work for the girls who made up most of the workforce. At least on an assembly line, you could chat to the next worker or let your mind wander while you carried out a repetitive task. Mechanised clerical work demanded total attention, but granted no intellectual satisfaction in return."* — Ch.1

Simmons began to dream:
> *"Machines would be invented which would be capable of doing all this work automatically. Such machines would free managers such as himself from marshalling their armies of clerks, and allow them to examine the figures, to digest them, and to learn from them what they had to tell us of better ways to conduct the company's business."* — Ch.1

## Were people called "computers"?
**Yes — confirmed.** In wartime, teams of women doing numerical calculations were literally called "human computors" (the contemporary spelling):

> *"Teams of women — 'human computors' — were being trained to calculate firing tables for artillery using mechanical desk calculators."* — Ch.2, describing the Moore School of Engineering during WWII

At Lyons they were called "clerks", but the function was identical: human beings doing machine arithmetic, all day, every day.

## The scaling wall
By the 1930s–40s Lyons was hitting a wall. You could not grow the business without growing the clerical army proportionally. More teashops = more bills = more clerks. More clerks = more cost = smaller margins. The company was collapsing under its own administrative weight.

The only solution available before computers: even more clerks, even more adding machines, even more partitions.

## The Checking Department disappears
A twist: by 1945, wartime labour shortages forced Lyons to convert teashops to self-service cafeterias. No waitresses = no waitresses' bills = the Checking Department ceased to exist. The job Simmons had dreamed of automating had been abolished by the war.

But his vision had already fired the imagination of his colleagues. The *idea* survived even after the specific job disappeared.
